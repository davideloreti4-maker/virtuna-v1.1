### 1. First-Namer Wins (Frame Installation)
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Whoever names the situation first controls all behavior downstream — not because they are right, but because the nervous system universally prefers early structure. The brain organizes behavior around the first category it receives, before any debate about content begins. Arguments that arrive after the frame lands are automatically downstream of the decision, making them decorative rather than functional.
- **Source anchor:** "Whoever decides what kind of thing this is controls everything downstream... our behavior organizes itself around that first decision."
- **Dark flag:** covert-control — framing is installed without the target's awareness or consent; they experience it as obvious reality, not as a choice being made for them.
- **Detect-triple:** *Signal:* Creator opens video by declaring what KIND of content/experience this is ("This is the video that changed everything for me," "This is a reality check," "This is a tutorial, not a rant") before any content lands. · *Mechanism:* Nervous system locks onto first category; viewer filters all subsequent content through that lens — objections, boredom, or doubt reclassify as inappropriate before they fully form. · *Strong:* declarative label delivered calmly within first 3 seconds, no justification offered / *Mid:* label present but hedged or delayed past 5s / *Weak:* no framing — viewer assigns their own category.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes

### 2. Frame vs. Content — Never Argue Content
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** All persuasion attempts that argue facts, evidence, or conclusions operate INSIDE an already-established frame — making them structurally incapable of winning. The frame decides what counts as reasonable, reckless, or immoral. Changing the frame collapses all prior objections without addressing a single one. Hughes's rule: "never attack content, always attack context."
- **Source anchor:** "If you're arguing content, you're validating the frame."
- **Dark flag:** none
- **Detect-triple:** *Signal:* Creator recontextualizes a competing narrative or criticism by labeling the CONTEXT rather than rebutting specific claims ("This whole conversation is really about…" / "What's actually happening here is…"). · *Mechanism:* Viewer's existing objections lose coherence because the category they lived in no longer applies; they follow the new context rather than re-litigating facts. · *Strong:* explicit context-shift delivered early, competitor's frame never directly engaged / *Mid:* partial reframe mixed with factual rebuttals / *Weak:* pure fact-vs-fact counter.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes

### 3. Frame Tempo — Altitude Before Content
- **Tag:** content-lever
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Every interaction begins at an emotional altitude — high (abstract, clinical, slow) or low (urgent, reactive, personal). Whoever controls altitude controls tempo. The first move is never about content; it is about where the conversation is happening from. If the room is emotionally hot, the operator rises rather than dives in — widening the view, slowing the pace, naming structure instead of matching pressure.
- **Source anchor:** "Whoever controls altitude controls tempo. That's why the first move you will make is never ever about content."
- **Dark flag:** none
- **Detect-triple:** *Signal:* Video opens at a noticeably different register than the topic demands — unusually calm delivery on an emotionally charged topic, or an abstract framing sentence before diving into specific content. · *Mechanism:* Altitude signals authority and control; viewer's nervous system reads the operator as stabilizing rather than reactive, increasing trust and compliance. · *Strong:* deliberate register mismatch that creates authority contrast / *Mid:* calm delivery but no explicit altitude-setting / *Weak:* creator matches emotional tone of the subject.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes

### 4. Four-Step Frame Installation (Boring = Strong)
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Frames are installed by: (1) naming early, (2) naming calmly, (3) naming without justification, (4) naming as if it were obvious all along. The more boring and self-evident the frame sounds, the more powerful it is — because a frame that draws attention to itself invites rejection. Flowery or elaborate framing places it on a platter for the listener to refuse.
- **Source anchor:** "The more boring it sounds, the stronger the frame is. Always remember, if I'm calling attention with flowery language for the frame, I'm automatically putting it up on a platter to see if somebody can reject it."
- **Dark flag:** covert-control — explicit design goal is for the target NOT to notice the installation.
- **Detect-triple:** *Signal:* Creator states the type or purpose of the video in a flat, unjustified throwaway clause ("Obviously this is just about…" / "This is really a creativity conversation"). · *Mechanism:* Absence of justification signals certainty; the brain treats unjustified assertions as ambient fact rather than claims requiring evaluation. · *Strong:* boring/flat delivery, zero defense of the label / *Mid:* calm but with slight over-explanation / *Weak:* creator pitches or justifies why the video is categorized that way.

### 9. Frame Defanging — Surface, Don't Argue
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** To neutralize a hostile frame, never argue inside it — that validates it. Instead: (1) distance the frame from the person (it's a common pattern, not their fault), (2) name the structure (label what the frame IS doing), (3) externalize the consequences (let the pattern do the arguing), (4) offer an exit without installing a replacement yet. The frame loses automation the moment it becomes consciously visible, because unconscious frames require no maintenance but conscious frames collapse under scrutiny.
- **Source anchor:** "The moment that person's frame becomes visible, it loses automation... frames are automatic until they are observed. Once observed, they require attention and maintenance, which they never required if they're in the unconscious."
- **Dark flag:** none — this is defensive technique; can also be used offensively to dismantle others' framing.
- **Detect-triple:** *Signal:* Creator explicitly names and labels a competitor's or critic's framing pattern ("I notice this conversation always starts with X assumption" / "There's a common frame that shows up here") without attacking the person. · *Mechanism:* Making the frame visible transfers it from automatic to effortful; the original framer loses control and the audience experiences relief + intellectual permission. · *Strong:* four-step defang sequence complete, exit offered / *Mid:* names frame but argues content anyway / *Weak:* argues inside the competitor's frame.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes

### 10. Frame Replacement — Let the Old One Die
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Replacing a frame is NOT pitching the new one. The error is selling or justifying the replacement. The correct move is soft substitution: allow the old frame to collapse, then offer the new category quietly as the only reasonable option remaining. The replacement should feel inevitable, not argued. Key failure modes: over-explaining, moralizing, sounding clever, rushing the exit, needing to win.
- **Source anchor:** "What you should be doing is you let it feel like the only reasonable option... you let the old one kind of die off and then you're offering a quieter, little safer alternative."
- **Dark flag:** covert-control — the technique is explicitly designed so the target experiences the new frame as self-chosen rather than installed.
- **Detect-triple:** *Signal:* Creator doesn't argue for their position; they let a prior narrative visibly fail (via its own consequences or absurdity), then calmly introduce an alternative without selling it. · *Mechanism:* Void created by collapsed frame is filled by the first available alternative; offering it quietly positions it as discovery, not persuasion. · *Strong:* visible death of old frame + understated introduction of new one / *Mid:* new frame introduced but slightly over-explained / *Weak:* creator pitches the new frame with enthusiasm.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes

### 13. Name Function, Not Trait
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** The mechanism for clean identity substitution is naming what a behavior IS DOING (its function/purpose) rather than what the person IS (their trait). "That reaction makes sense as a protective move" names function — it does not call the person "protective." Function-naming allows the identity to reorganize on its own, without the person feeling diagnosed or labeled. This is the technical trick for all metaphor work.
- **Source anchor:** "What you're naming is a function, not a trait. That's the trick. And that's the trick for all of these. We're naming the function of what's happening instead of traits of what people are doing."
- **Dark flag:** none — this is a precision frame; can be used therapeutically or manipulatively depending on intent.
- **Detect-triple:** *Signal:* Creator reframes viewer behavior or emotion by explaining WHY it's occurring (system response, protection mechanism, load management) rather than labeling the viewer as a type of person. · *Mechanism:* Functional framing implies agency and intelligence in the viewer's behavior; they feel understood rather than categorized, increasing trust and receptivity. · *Strong:* pure function-naming, no trait language / *Mid:* mixes function and trait ("you're a protective person who…") / *Weak:* pure trait-labeling.
