### 1. The Six-Axis Model of Influence
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Every influence interaction can be mapped along six orthogonal axes: Suggestibility, Focus, Openness, Connection, Compliance, Expectancy. These are not traits but *states* — they fluctuate within a conversation. Crucially, all six are not required; only three of the six need to be elevated to a high degree to make persuasion succeed.
- **Source anchor:** "in reality only three of these qualities need to be present for somebody to be persuaded but they do have to be present to a pretty large degree … you can literally pick any three."
- **Dark flag:** covert-control — the model is designed to be used covertly to diagnose and steer without the target's awareness.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — the "any 3 of 6" threshold, not the full set, is the actionable insight.

---

### 2. Suggestibility vs. Compliance Are Distinct Levers
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Suggestibility is the degree to which a person will accept and act on an indirect *suggestion*. Compliance is the degree to which they will obey a direct *command or direction*. These are cognitively separate pathways: a person can be highly compliant (obedient to orders) yet low in suggestibility (resistant to ideas framed indirectly), or vice versa. Conflating them wastes leverage.
- **Source anchor:** "suggestibility and compliance are different things. Suggestibility is responding to a suggestion. Compliance is responding to an actual direction or command to do something."
- **Dark flag:** covert-control — understanding the split enables precision targeting of whichever pathway is open, without the target recognising the mechanism.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — most practitioners treat these as one "persuadability" variable.

---

### 3. Expectancy as a Persuasion Axis
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Expectancy is the degree to which a person can *predict* (consciously or unconsciously) what will happen next *and* whether it will be enjoyable. When expectancy is high, anxiety drops, trust rises, and the other axes become easier to elevate. When expectancy is absent (novelty, uncertainty), the brain's threat-detection system dominates and persuasion is harder — unless combined with authority to override it.
- **Source anchor:** "the degree to which a person reasonably feels that they can predict either consciously or unconsciously what's going to be happening soon and whether or not it's going to be enjoyed."
- **Dark flag:** manipulation — deliberately *removing* expectancy (unpredictability) can be used to increase suggestibility and compliance via anxiety, as seen in the Milgram context where lack of expectancy amplified obedience.
- **If content-lever or both — draft detect-triple:**  
  *Signal:* Creator structures the video with clear signposted segments ("first … next … finally"), promises a payoff upfront, or uses pattern/rule reveals the viewer can predict will deliver value.  
  *Mechanism:* Predictable structure lowers threat-detection load; viewer stays engaged expecting the reward rather than scanning for exit.  
  *Strong / Mid / Weak:* Strong = explicit promise + delivered payoff with matched structure; Mid = implicit structure but no payoff cue; Weak = random cut/jump cuts with no through-line.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — expectancy is usually framed as "hooks," but Hughes frames it as a *trust axis* that modulates the entire persuasion field, not just attention.

### 4. The "Any Three" Threshold — Persuasion Is Over-Determined
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Because any three of the six axes will produce persuasion when sufficiently elevated, an operator does not need to achieve full-spectrum rapport. This makes persuasion robust and fault-tolerant: deficits in connection or openness (hard to build quickly) can be compensated by maximising focus, suggestibility, and compliance (easier to engineer quickly via authority, novelty, framing). This also explains why seemingly cold or impersonal contexts (Milgram lab, telemarketing calls) can still achieve profound compliance.
- **Source anchor:** "even with only three qualities in the green here just three out of the six the experiment produced profound results where people deviated dramatically from their normal social behaviors."
- **Dark flag:** covert-control — the redundancy makes covert influence especially hard to defend against; blocking one axis (e.g., refusing connection) does not neutralise the other five.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — implies a practitioner should audit *which three* are most accessible in a given context rather than trying to max all six.

### 5. Focus Is the Primary Gate — Ancestor Scripts Trigger It
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Focus (attention maintenance, exclusion of competing stimuli) is activated at an almost involuntary level by what Hughes calls "Fate model" / ancestor scripts — evolutionary threat and novelty responses inherited from pre-human ancestors. Authority figures and novel situations both trip these scripts automatically, hijacking focus without the target's consent. Once focus is captured, the remaining axes become easier to elevate.
- **Source anchor:** "they were hyper focused on the situation due to the ancestor scripts … that were triggered both with the authority that was present and the novelty or newness of the situation."
- **Dark flag:** manipulation — framing content/interaction to mimic authority signals or inject controlled novelty is a covert focus-capture mechanism.
- **If content-lever or both — draft detect-triple:**  
  *Signal:* Video opens with a credible authority signal (credentials, confident framing, insider language) OR an unexpected/novel visual or claim that breaks pattern expectation.  
  *Mechanism:* Ancestor script activates; viewer's threat/novelty detection locks focus involuntarily before conscious evaluation.  
  *Strong / Mid / Weak:* Strong = genuine authority cue + genuine novelty in first 3s; Mid = one of the two without the other; Weak = generic opener, no authority or novelty trigger.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — the mechanism is *involuntary* and *pre-conscious*, meaning the viewer cannot opt out even if aware of it.

### 6. Life Scripts vs. Ancestor Scripts — Overwriting Core Identity
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Hughes distinguishes *life scripts* (culturally/personally formed values like "don't kill people") from *ancestor scripts* (evolutionary behavioral programs). When authority and focus combine to elevate compliance and suggestibility, ancestor scripts can *trump* life scripts entirely — producing behavior the person would consciously consider monstrous. The Milgram participants who "shocked" strangers had their life-script ("don't harm innocents") overridden by an ancestor-script (obey hierarchical authority + stay focused on the task).
- **Source anchor:** "The Rock Solid internal compass that we have that says don't kill people … was completely overwritten because it was trumped by an ancestor script."
- **Dark flag:** covert-control — this is the mechanism behind cult induction, radicalization, and high-compliance sales. Knowing which ancestor script overrides which life script is a blueprint for identity-level manipulation.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — most influence frameworks treat personal values as the floor; Hughes argues they are *ceilings* that ancestor scripts routinely break through.

### 7. Openness Requires Absence of Social/Societal Repercussion Fear
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Openness — vulnerability and self-disclosure — only becomes available when a person perceives zero social cost for being exposed. This means creators or operators must proactively *remove* the threat of judgment, not merely request trust. Vulnerability is not an attitude; it is an output of perceived safety from social consequences.
- **Source anchor:** "the degree to which a person feels they can just completely open up and become vulnerable … typically marked by an absence of a fear of social or societal repercussions."
- **Dark flag:** manipulation — creating artificial "safe zones" (confessional tone, in-group framing, "just between us" language) can be used to induce disclosure the person would never offer in a normal social context.
- **If content-lever or both — draft detect-triple:**  
  *Signal:* Creator explicitly normalizes the viewer's hidden struggle, names a taboo, or signals "no judgment here" before asking for emotional engagement or a purchase decision.  
  *Mechanism:* Removes perceived social cost of admitting a need/problem; viewer's openness axis rises, making them receptive to the creator's framing of a solution.  
  *Strong / Mid / Weak:* Strong = specific named taboo + explicit safe-zone framing before CTA; Mid = generic empathy statement; Weak = straight value claim with no openness priming.
- **Non-obvious?:** no — "make people feel safe" is common advice, but the *mechanism* (fear removal, not rapport addition) is under-specified in standard frameworks.

---

### 8. Connection Is Marked by Desire for Future Contact
- **Tag:** both
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** True social connection is not measured by warmth in the moment but by a functional output: the person *wants future interaction* with the operator. This is a behavioral test, not an emotional one. It means connection-building tactics that feel warm but don't generate a desire to return are functionally useless on the influence map.
- **Source anchor:** "the degree to which a person feels a sense of social connection with another person … typically marked by a desire for future contact with that operator."
- **Dark flag:** none
- **If content-lever or both — draft detect-triple:**  
  *Signal:* Video ends with a credible reason to return (serialized content, open loop, follow-up promise) that makes the viewer want the next installment — not just a "subscribe" CTA.  
  *Mechanism:* Converts momentary engagement into a desire-for-future-contact signal; connection axis registers as real.  
  *Strong / Mid / Weak:* Strong = compelling open loop + sincere next-episode promise; Mid = generic subscribe CTA; Weak = cold close, no reason to return.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — frames "follow" behavior as the *diagnostic output* of connection, not its cause.

### 9. Persuasion Defined as Deviation from Normal Behavior
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Hughes defines persuasion not as "convincing someone of something true" but as causing *any* behavior to occur that would never have occurred otherwise — across the entire spectrum from low-stakes (try a new restaurant) to high-stakes (exit an eating disorder in one therapy session). This frame decouples persuasion from ethics and from truthfulness; the only criterion is behavioral deviation.
- **Source anchor:** "persuasion is the action of causing a behavior to occur that would never have occurred otherwise."
- **Dark flag:** other — the definition is deliberately ethics-neutral. It frames manipulation and therapy as identical in mechanism, different only in application. This is useful for analysis but dangerous if used as a practitioner justification.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — standard persuasion frameworks anchor around belief change; Hughes anchors around *behavioral delta*, which is more precise and more measurable.

### 10. The Map Metaphor — Diagnosis + Planning Tool
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** The six-axis model is explicitly dual-use: (a) prospective — plan which axes to elevate before an interaction; (b) retrospective — diagnose which axes were present/absent when an influence attempt failed or succeeded. This gives it diagnostic and prognostic power that single-axis models (e.g., just "build rapport") lack. Every influence technique, body language read, or behavior-profiling insight maps back into one of the six axes.
- **Source anchor:** "the model can be used both as a planning tool to ensure a conversation is as persuasive as it can be and it could be used as a post-mortem tool to figure out what went wrong."
- **Dark flag:** none
- **Non-obvious?:** no — but the *universality claim* ("every influence technique you will ever learn pivots around one of these six axes") is a strong structural assertion worth testing.

### 11. Information ≠ Skill — Academic Study Produces Neither Charisma Nor Influence
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Hughes distinguishes between *knowing about* influence (information) and *being able to execute* influence (skill). Academics who study charisma for decades may have zero of it, because information without practice in real high-stakes conditions does not transfer to capability. The implication is that any AI reasoning about influence must model the skill gap, not just the knowledge state, when profiling creators or users.
- **Source anchor:** "information and skill are two very different things. The academic stuff was all about information instead of reality and I needed reality."
- **Dark flag:** none
- **Non-obvious?:** no — but relevant as a design principle for how Numen frames feedback (not "here is the principle" but "here is the gap between what you know and what you execute").

### 12. Authority + Novelty as a Dual-Key Focus Trigger
- **Tag:** content-lever
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Two independent stimuli each trigger the focus axis via ancestor scripts: (1) *perceived authority* (hierarchical status, expertise markers, lab-coat-equivalents) and (2) *novelty* (new, unexpected, pattern-breaking stimuli). When both are present simultaneously, focus is maximally captured. This is a combinatorial multiplier: a novel claim delivered by a credible authority figure is exponentially more attention-capturing than either alone.
- **Source anchor:** "they were hyper focused … due to the ancestor scripts … triggered both with the authority that was present and the novelty or newness of the situation."
- **Dark flag:** manipulation — combining authority signals with manufactured novelty is a standard attention-engineering technique used in advertising, propaganda, and cult recruitment.
- **If content-lever or both — draft detect-triple:**  
  *Signal:* Creator combines an authority marker (credential, track record, insider knowledge) with a genuinely surprising claim or visual in the same opening beat.  
  *Mechanism:* Dual-key ancestor-script activation; both authority and novelty circuits fire simultaneously, locking attention before analytical resistance engages.  
  *Strong / Mid / Weak:* Strong = both keys present in first 3s, claim is genuinely counter-intuitive; Mid = one key only; Weak = neither — generic opener with no status or surprise signal.
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — the *multiplicative* (not additive) relationship between authority and novelty as focus triggers is the non-obvious frame.

---

### 13. Persuasion Scale — Magnitude of Behavioral Deviation as Quality Metric
- **Tag:** reasoning-substrate
- **Mechanism (full fidelity):** Hughes implies a persuasion quality spectrum calibrated by *how far* the induced behavior deviates from the person's baseline. Low-scale persuasion = small deviation (try a different restaurant). High-scale persuasion = massive deviation (exit a multi-year eating disorder in one session). This scalar view means persuasion effectiveness can be graded, and it repositions high-conversion short-form content as occupying a specific band on this scale — not "just" marketing but a genuine behavioral override mechanism.
- **Source anchor:** "something low on the persuasion scale might be talking to a new friend … something high on the persuasion scale might be a therapist using techniques to talk somebody out of eating disorder in a single session."
- **Dark flag:** none
- **Non-obvious?:** yes — grading persuasion by behavioral delta magnitude, not by topic importance, reframes what "effective" content actually means.
